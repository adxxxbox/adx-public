### One-Lung Ventilation Notes:

Edited by Kyle J. Crawford DNP, CRNA, APRN

Adjunct Professor at University of Tennessee at Chattanooga

**Pressure control ventilation (PCV):** Consider it for preventing barotrauma in the ventilated (non-collapsed) lung.

**Tidal volume:** 4-6 mL/kg

_**Note:** Normal tidal volume for two ventilated lungs: 6-8 mL/kg of ideal body weight._

**Permissive hypercapnia** (PaCO2 50-60 mmHg) is usually acceptable, except for patients with pulmonary hypertension.

**PEEP:** 3-8 cm H2O, unless there is a bronchopleural fistula.

**FiO2** 0.6-1.0

**PIP:** < 35 cm H2O

**Plateau pressure:** < 25 cm H2O

**Hypoxic Pulmonary Vasoconstriction (HPV) Reflex:**

-   A physiological reflex where the pulmonary arterioles constrict in response to low oxygen levels in the alveoli.
-   This redirection of blood flow away from poorly ventilated lung regions to the better-ventilated areas optimize oxygen delivery and help maintain overall systemic oxygenation.

**HPV occurs in two parts:**

**The initial phase** (within minutes and plateaus by 30 minutes)

**Delayed phase** (starts at ~40 mins and plateaus after a few hours).

Several anesthetic factors can increase or decrease this reflex.

**Several factors can influence/potentiate HPV:**

-   Oxygen tension: Low oxygen tension in the alveoli is the primary stimulus for HPV.
-   CO2 levels and ↓ pH (respiratory acidosis)
-   Metabolic acidosis
-   Mild decreased mixed venous oxygenation
-   Sympathetic nervous system activity will ↑ HPV
-   Mild decreased mixed venous oxygenation
-   Hypothermia (low body temperature) inhibits HPV
-   Hyperthermia enhances it.
-   **Note:** Hypothermia itself is associated with a significant increase in pulmonary vascular resistance.

Inhibiting HPV can potentially lead to decreased oxygen delivery and worsen gas exchange.

**Agents/factors that decrease PVR inhibiting HPV:**

-   Inhaled Nitric oxide (NO)
-   Nitrous oxide (N₂O)
-   Prostacyclin analogs for PHT
-   Phosphodiester (type 5) inhibitors (Cialis and Viagra) for PHT
-   Calcium channel blockers-Nicardipine, Nifedipine and Diltiazem
-   Nitric oxide donors: Nitroglycerine and Nitroprusside
-   ACE inhibitors
-   Milrinone
-   Increased left atria (LA) pressure -see notes at the bottom of this page
-   Hemodilution (fluid overload- limit fluids)
-   Metabolic and respiratory alkalosis

**Medications that will NOT inhibit HPV:**

Alpha-1 agonist phenylephrine and norepinephrine

**Hypoxia with OLV:**

Incidence of SaO2 < 90% occurs ~5% of the time and is usually a result of shunting.

**Several factors predict the occurrence of hypoxia during OLV:**

-   Low PO2 prior to OLV
-   Left-sided ventilation (right thoracotomy)
-   Compromised FEV1 or FVC or restrictive lung disease
-   Robotic thymectomies in the supine position often experience ventilation/perfusion (V/Q) to the operative lung.

**Troubleshooting and treatment for desaturations during OLV:**

-   First, remember the factors that affect the HPV reflex!
-   SaO2 may initially drop and then spontaneously return to normal levels.
-   Increase the FIO2 to 100%, if not already.
-   Consider adding additional PEEP (5-10 cm H2O) to the dependent lung.
-   Ensure the inhalation agent is <1 MAC.
-   Confirm DLT placement with a FOB - one leading cause of desaturation is DLT displacement when moving the patient laterally or undergoing surgical manipulation.
-   Assess cardiac output (CO) to ensure it is adequate (surgical manipulation may temporarily decrease CO).
-   CPAP (5-10 cm H2O) can be applied to the nondependent lung to increase PaO2.
-   A differential diagnosis could be a pneumothorax, especially in patients with COPD or the presence of blebs/bullae.
-   If the patient’s SaO2 is continuing to drop, consult with the surgeon to temporarily reinflate the collapsed lung and perform recruitment measures.
-   Do not be afraid to inform the surgeon as recruiting the independent lung due to refractory desaturations are not uncommon.
