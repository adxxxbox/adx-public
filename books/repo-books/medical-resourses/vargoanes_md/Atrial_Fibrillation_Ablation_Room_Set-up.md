### Atrial Fibrillation Ablation Room Set-up

General anesthesia with ETT

**Arterial line set up** and primed for ACT draws

**500 mL Heparin** bag and infusion tubing primed -connect to a pump

250 mL bag of **Norepinephrine** or **Neosynephrine** pressor bag for standby

**Isuprel bag (50 cc)** primed and connected to a pump (only when requested by cardiologist).

Syringes:

1 cc syringes for ACT’s

10 cc syringe of pressor (most anesthesia providers prefer **norepinephrine**)

10 cc syringe of **heparin** (1,000 units/mL)

5 cc syringe of **protamine** (50 mg)-keep separated from other syringes

Induction and muscle relaxant syringes

Narcotic (2 cc of **fentanyl** is common)

**Decadron and Zofran:** If not contraindicated

**Esophageal temperature probe:** Some cardiologists will prefer a pediatric probe.

Arterial line set up:

Despite these patients rarely requiring an A-line, we commonly use an arterial line set-up for venous samples from a venous catheter in the groin that the cardiologist inserts.

The EP team will hand you an extension tubing from the sterile field for you to connect the A-line to flush the air out (when they are setting up).

Many anesthesia providers will tape the arterial line transducer to the anesthesia table top for easier access when drawing ACTs.

Isuprel bag:

Only with a cardiologist's request - to be used at the end of the procedure.

0.2 mg in a 50 cc bag (0.4 mg/mL)

Refer to the Isuprel Drip Rate table in “Pulsed Field Ablation for Atrial Fibrillation.”

**Protamine:** It is always safe to keep protamine away from the field (table-top) to prevent accidental administration.
