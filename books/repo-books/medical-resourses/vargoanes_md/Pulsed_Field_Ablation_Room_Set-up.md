### Pulsed Field Ablation Room Set-up

**Arterial line set up** and primed for ACT draws

**500 mL Heparin** bag and infusion tubing primed -connect to a pump

250 mL bag of **Norepinephrine** or **Neosynephrine** pressor bag for standby

**Nitroglycerine bottle:** Do not open until requested by cardiologist

**Isuprel bag (50 cc)** primed and connected to a pump (only when requested by cardiologist).

Syringes:

1 cc syringes for ACT’s

3 cc syringe of 0.4 mg **Atropine**

10 cc syringe of pressor (most anesthesia providers prefer **norepinephrine**)

10 cc syringe of **heparin** (1,000 units/mL)

5 cc syringe of **protamine** (50 mg)-keep separated from other syringes

Induction and muscle relaxant syringes

Narcotic (2 cc of **fentanyl** is common)

**Decadron and Zofran**, if not contraindicated

**Esophageal temperature probe:** Is NOT needed for this procedure.

However, they are required for the traditional Atrial Fib ablation procedures.

Arterial line set up:

Although these patients rarely require an A-line, we commonly use an arterial line set-up for venous ACT samples from a venous catheter in the groin, which the cardiologist inserts.

The EP team will hand you an extension tubing from the sterile field for you to connect the A-line to, then flush the air out (when they are setting up).

Many anesthesia providers will tape the arterial line transducer to the anesthesia table top for easier access when drawing ACTs.

3 cc syringe of 0.4 mg Atropine:

Possible asystole with the first pulse ablation.

The cardiologist will inform you when to administer it.

Isuprel bag:

Only with a cardiologist's request - to be used at the end of the procedure.

0.2 mg in a 50 mL bag (0.4 mg/mL)

Refer to the Isuprel Drip Rate table in _“Pulsed Field Ablation for Atrial Fibrillation.”_

**Nitroglycerine (NTG) bottle:** Do not open the bottle until requested.

For patients who have a right-dominant coronary artery system.

Ablation to the cavo-tricuspid isthmus (CTI) may cause vasospasm in the right coronary artery (RCA) and circumflex artery.

\- To prevent coronary vasospasm, a very large dose (1-3 mg) of NGT is to be administered just before ablating the CTI.

Norepinephrine or phenylephrine bag:

Prior to bolusing NTG, you will need to build up the patient’s BP to prevent severe hypotension from the bolus of nitroglycerine.

**Protamine:** It is always safe to keep protamine away from the field (table-top) to prevent accidental administration.
