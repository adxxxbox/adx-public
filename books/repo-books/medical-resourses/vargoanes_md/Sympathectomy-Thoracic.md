### Thoracic Sympathectomy

-   A Thoracic Sympathectomy is now usually performed via a minimally invasive (VATS) procedure.
-   The sympathetic chain inside the chest at the level of T2, T3, and T4 is either cauterized, dissected, excised, or clamped.
-   This procedure is usually performed bilaterally.

**Indications:**

-   Hyperhidrosis
-   Causalgia syndrome
-   Reflex sympathetic dystrophy of the upper extremities or Renauld’s.

**Procedure:**

-   A small incision is made near the tip of the shoulder blade or under the armpit for the insertion of the endoscope into the chest between the ribs.
-   The lung is retracted out of the way, and the sympathetic nerves are visualized with the endoscope.
-   Through another small incision, a small instrument is introduced between the ribs to divide the sympathetic nerves at the level of the second and third ribs.
-   The results of the sympathectomy are apparent within minutes after the surgery.

**Anesthetic:** General/ETT (single or DLT, depending on surgeon preference).

**Position:** Either supine or lateral.

_If lateral position with a DLT, refer to “Thoracotomy and Positioning” and_

_“Thoracoscopy-One Lung Ventilation (OLV) with and Hypoxic Pulmonary Vasoconstriction (HPV).”_

**Duration:** 1-3 hours

**Epidural:** Usually for post op pain

**A-line:** Yes

**EBL:** < 50mLs

**IV Access:** One 18G

**Ventilation:** If the surgeon prefers a single-lumen ETT, you will still experience elevated peak inspiratory pressures due to the lung being displaced.

You may need to use pressure-controlled ventilation.

**Complications:**

Hemorrhage

Horner Syndrome (ptosis, miosis, enophthalmos and anhydrosis)

Vocal Cord Damage from the DLT insertion

Infection

Trauma to surrounding organs

Pneumothorax
